Carbon dots (CDs) were synthesized by microwave irradiation of glucose and tryptophan, with the reaction conditions optimized using response surface methodology. The synthesis involved $2.5\mathrm{g}$ glucose and $300\mathrm{mg}$ of tryptophan in $15\mathrm{mL}$ of water, exposed to 700 W microwave radiation for $5\mathrm{min}$. The resulting Trp-CDs had a fluorescence quantum yield of $12.4\%$ and an average size of $20\mathrm{nm}$, with fluorescence quenching observed upon exposure to $\mathsf{O N O O^{-}}$. The sensor showed a linear detection range of $5 \upmu \mathrm{M}$ to $25 \upmu \mathrm{M}$, with a limit of detection of $1.5 \upmu \mathrm{M}$.
A simple fluorescent sensor array for detecting toxic metal ions was developed using nitrogen-doped carbon dots (N-CDs). These were synthesized via hydrothermal treatment of citric acid with amino acids like glycine, lysine, and serine. The N-CDs exhibited distinct quenching responses to six metal ions, enabling their differentiation at concentrations as low as $10 \upmu \mathrm{M}$. The method is simple, label-free, and highly accurate, achieving $100\%$ accuracy in distinguishing unknown samples.
A fluorescent sensor array was created for detecting toxic metal ions by synthesizing three types of nitrogen-doped carbon dots using citric acid and different amino acids (glycine, lysine, and serine). The sensor could differentiate six metal ions at concentrations ranging from 100 μM to 10 μM, with 100% accuracy. This method can also identify binary and ternary mixtures of metal ions, offering a low-cost, scalable, and sensitive detection system for environmental monitoring.

Synthesis of Carbon Quantum Dots: Carbon Quantum Dots (CQDs) were synthesized using a microwave-assisted method by irradiating a mixture of glucose (2.5 g) and tryptophan (300 mg) in 15 mL of water, exposed to 700 W microwave power for 5 min. The CQDs had a fluorescence quantum yield of 12.4% and an average diameter of 20 nm, exhibiting fluorescence quenching in the presence of ONOO^-. The detection range for ONOO^- was linear between 5 μM and 25 μM with a limit of detection of 1.5 μM.
